# 1. CORE IDEAS (Compressed Understanding)

## Central Problem

* Are individuals **fully responsible** for their actions?
* Tension:

  * **Radical freedom (Sartre)**
  * vs
  * **Social constraints (Oshana)**

---

## SARTRE: EXISTENTIALISM

### Core Claim

* **“Existence precedes essence”**
  → Humans:
* Are not born with a fixed nature
* Create themselves through choices 

---

### Key Concepts

#### Freedom

* Humans are **radically free**
* Always able to choose (even not choosing is a choice)

#### Responsibility

* You are responsible for:

  * Your actions
  * What you become
  * Humanity (your choices model others)

→ “In choosing myself, I choose humanity” 

---

#### Authenticity

* Living in recognition of your freedom

#### Bad Faith

* Self-deception:

  * Denying freedom
  * Hiding behind roles or excuses 

---

#### Transcendence vs Facticity

* **Transcendence**:

  * Freedom, possibilities, choice
* **Facticity**:

  * Constraints (body, past, society)

→ Human condition = tension between both 

---

## OSHANA: SOCIAL AUTONOMY

### Core Claim

* Autonomy is **not just internal**
* Requires **external conditions**

---

### Internalist Account (IA)

* Autonomy =:

  * Rational reflection
  * Authentic values

---

### Externalist Account (EA) (Oshana)

* Autonomy requires:

  * Control over life circumstances
  * Social conditions enabling choice 

---

### Key Idea

* You can:

  * Choose freely psychologically
  * BUT still lack autonomy due to social constraints

---

### Four Conditions of Autonomy

1. Critical reflection
2. Procedural independence
3. Access to real options
4. Social-relational conditions 

---

## KEY TENSION

| Sartre                      | Oshana                            |
| --------------------------- | --------------------------------- |
| Radical freedom             | Constrained autonomy              |
| Responsibility always total | Responsibility depends on context |
| Individual focus            | Social structure focus            |

# 2. ARGUMENT MAPS

## SARTRE: RADICAL FREEDOM

### Thesis

* Humans are **always free and fully responsible**

---

### Argument Structure

1. There is no fixed human nature
2. Humans define themselves through choices
3. Every action reflects a chosen value
4. Therefore:

   * Humans are fully responsible for their actions

---

### Key Extension

* Your choice:

  * Implies a view of how humans should act
    → Universal responsibility

---

### Assumptions

* Freedom is always possible
* Constraints never eliminate choice
* Consciousness is not determined

---

### Strengths

* Emphasizes moral responsibility
* Avoids determinism
* Explains guilt, anxiety (“vertigo”)

---

### Weaknesses

#### 1. Overly Demanding

* Blames individuals for everything

#### 2. Ignores Social Constraints

* Poverty, oppression, coercion

#### 3. Unrealistic Freedom

* Not all options are genuinely available

---

### Counterarguments

* Social determinism
* Psychological constraints
* Structural injustice

---

## BAD FAITH ARGUMENT

### Structure

1. Humans are free
2. Freedom creates anxiety
3. People deny freedom to escape anxiety
4. This denial = bad faith

---

### Example

* “I had no choice” = denial of responsibility

---

## OSHANA: SOCIAL AUTONOMY

### Thesis

* Autonomy requires **external conditions**, not just internal reflection

---

### Argument Structure

1. Internalist view:

   * Autonomy = rational choice
2. But:

   * People can rationally choose under oppression
3. Yet:

   * They are not truly self-governing
4. Therefore:

   * Autonomy requires external freedom

---

### Key Examples

#### Voluntary Slave

* Chooses slavery
* IA: autonomous
* EA: not autonomous

---

#### Subservient Woman

* Reflective and consistent
* But socially constrained
  → Not autonomous 

---

#### Conscientious Objector

* Rejects authority, chooses prison
  → Autonomous (resists domination)

---

### Assumptions

* Social conditions shape agency
* Freedom requires real options

---

### Strengths

* Realistic account of autonomy
* Accounts for oppression
* Explains unequal freedom

---

### Weaknesses

* Risk of paternalism:

  * Declaring others “not autonomous”
* Hard to define “sufficient conditions”

# 3. ESSAY-READY FRAMES

## Q1. Are humans fully responsible for their actions?

### Thesis

* Humans are responsible for their choices, but responsibility is constrained by social conditions.

---

### Outline

* Sartre: radical responsibility
* Argument: existence precedes essence
* Problem: ignores constraints
* Oshana: externalist autonomy
* Conclusion: qualified responsibility

---

### Mistakes

* Saying Sartre denies constraints (he doesn’t—he reinterprets them)
* Ignoring autonomy debate

---

## Q2. Is Sartre’s concept of freedom plausible?

### Thesis

* Sartre’s account captures moral responsibility but overestimates human freedom.

---

## Q3. What is bad faith and why is it important?

### Thesis

* Bad faith explains how individuals evade responsibility, but may overlook genuine constraints.

---

## Q4. Does autonomy require social conditions?

### Thesis

* Yes, because without real options, internal freedom is insufficient for self-governance.

---

## Q5. Compare Sartre and Oshana on freedom.

### Thesis

* Sartre emphasizes individual freedom, while Oshana shows that freedom is socially structured.

# 6. CONNECTIONS & INSIGHT

## Sartre vs Wittgenstein

* Sartre: freedom defines meaning
* Wittgenstein: meaning defined by use

---

## Sartre vs Transhumanism

* Sartre: humans define themselves
* Transhumanism: tech reshapes what humans are

---

## Sartre vs Kant

* Kant: duty + rational law
* Sartre: no fixed moral law → radical responsibility

---

## Big Insight

Freedom is:

* Not just:

  * Ability to choose (Sartre)
* But also:

  * Ability to **actually act on choices** (Oshana)

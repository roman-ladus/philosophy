# Why are freedom and responsibility important in Sartre’s existentialism, and how might Oshana’s account of autonomy challenge his position? Do you agree more with Sartre or Oshana? Explain your answer, with examples, ensuring that you also explain what is meant by freedom, responsibility, and autonomy.

**Freedom, Responsibility, and Autonomy: Sartre and Oshana**

Jean-Paul Sartre’s existentialism places freedom and responsibility at the center of human life because, on his view, there is no fixed human essence, divine plan, or objective moral script to determine what we should be. To be human is to be *free*: not free in the trivial sense of always getting what one wants, but free in the deeper sense of having to choose oneself through action. With this freedom comes *responsibility*, since each choice expresses a commitment for which no external authority can ultimately be blamed. Marina Oshana’s account of *autonomy*, however, challenges Sartre by arguing that genuine self-government depends not only on inner choice but also on social and relational conditions that enable a person to direct her own life. My thesis is that Sartre is right to emphasize the inescapability of choice and the moral seriousness of responsibility, but Oshana offers the more convincing overall account because she shows that freedom without adequate social power is too thin to count as full autonomy.

Sartre’s famous claim that “existence precedes essence” means that human beings are not born with a predetermined nature that dictates their values or ends. Unlike a knife, whose essence is fixed by its function, a person first exists and then becomes what she is through her projects. *Freedom*, then, is the condition of not being fully determined by prior causes, roles, or essences in the practical sphere of action. Even in constraining circumstances, Sartre thinks, one must choose how to respond. This is why he says we are “condemned to be free”: freedom is not a privilege but a burden. *Responsibility* follows because if my actions are genuinely mine, then I cannot excuse them by saying “I had no choice” whenever alternatives, attitudes, or refusals remained possible. Sartre’s target is bad faith, the attempt to flee this burden by pretending that one is merely a waiter, a coward, or a social role rather than a chooser.

This emphasis is philosophically important because it restores agency against both determinism and moral complacency. Sartre sees that people often hide behind convention, psychology, or authority to avoid self-scrutiny. In this respect, he resembles Kant, for whom autonomy requires acting from principles one can endorse rather than from heteronomous pressures. Sartre’s version is more radical: there is no universal rational law already given, so each person must create value through commitment. A student deciding whether to care for his mother or join the resistance, one of Sartre’s well-known examples, cannot rely on a moral algorithm. Whatever he chooses, he authors himself through that act. Sartre is therefore right that responsibility is not added to freedom from outside; it is internal to what freedom is. To choose is already to answer for oneself.

Yet Oshana’s account of autonomy exposes a major weakness in Sartre. For Oshana, autonomy is not just an internal condition of reflective endorsement or choice; it is a socially situated capacity for self-direction. A person is autonomous only if she has sufficient authority, independence, and access to meaningful options within her social world. This matters because one can “choose” under domination in a way that still falls short of governing one’s life. Consider a woman raised in an oppressive household who sincerely endorses total obedience because alternatives have been systematically hidden or punished. Sartre might insist that she remains free and responsible, since she still chooses her stance toward her condition. Oshana would reply that this misses something crucial: if her social relations deprive her of real control over the shape of her life, then calling her fully autonomous is misleading. Freedom in Sartre’s sense may survive, but autonomy in the fuller sense does not.

This challenge is powerful because it distinguishes existential responsibility from substantive self-rule. Mill argued that liberty matters because individuals develop through experiments in living, but such development presupposes education, protection from coercion, and social space for individuality. Aristotle similarly held that flourishing requires external goods as well as virtue; practical reason cannot operate well under extreme deprivation. Oshana’s insight fits this tradition. It does not deny agency altogether, but it resists romanticizing it. A prisoner may be free in Sartre’s minimal sense to interpret imprisonment courageously, yet he is not autonomous with respect to the broader direction of his life. Oshana thus reveals that Sartre’s framework can obscure structural injustice by overemphasizing inward freedom.

A strong counterargument is that Oshana’s view risks making autonomy too dependent on external conditions and thereby diminishing personal responsibility. If autonomy requires favorable social standing or an adequate range of options, then many wrongdoers could claim reduced accountability by pointing to oppression, manipulation, or limited opportunities. Sartre’s harder line avoids this danger. It preserves the existential truth that, however constrained we are, we still participate in our actions. Even under pressure, one can resist, refuse, reinterpret, or accept. To forget this is to slide into a deterministic politics of victimhood.

This objection has force, but it does not defeat Oshana. The best reply is to distinguish *responsibility* from *autonomy* rather than collapsing them. A person may retain some responsibility because she acts intentionally, while lacking full autonomy because the surrounding conditions are domination-laden. Oshana does not have to say that oppressed agents are puppets; she can say instead that their agency is compromised. This is more plausible than Sartre’s near-totalization of freedom. Descartes thought the will was in some sense infinite, but contemporary moral reflection shows that social power shapes what subjects can know, imagine, and practically pursue. Recognizing this need not erase responsibility; it refines it.

I therefore agree more with Oshana, though not entirely against Sartre. Sartre is indispensable in showing that human beings cannot evade the normative significance of their choices. His account captures the lived experience of self-deception and the temptation to excuse oneself by appealing to role or circumstance. But Oshana is more convincing because she explains why not every act of choosing amounts to genuine self-government. Someone trafficked into labor, indoctrinated from childhood, or trapped in coercive dependence may still make choices, yet those choices are not enough for robust autonomy. A satisfactory view should preserve Sartre’s insistence on agency while adopting Oshana’s social realism about the conditions under which agency becomes truly one’s own.

In conclusion, freedom and responsibility are crucial for Sartre because they define what it is to be human in a world without pre-given meaning: we must choose, and in choosing we are accountable. Oshana challenges this by arguing that autonomy requires more than existential choice; it requires the social power to direct one’s life under conditions that support meaningful self-rule. Sartre illuminates the inner drama of agency, but Oshana better captures its real-world limits. The most persuasive position is therefore a qualified synthesis: Sartre is right that we are answerable for our choices, but Oshana is right that genuine autonomy depends on whether those choices are made from a position of actual self-direction rather than mere survival within constraint.

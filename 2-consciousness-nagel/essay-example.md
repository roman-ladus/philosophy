# Why does Nagel think subjective experience poses a problem for objective explanation? Do you agree with his position? Explain your answer, with examples, ensuring that you also explain what is meant by consciousness and “what it is like” to be something.

Thomas Nagel argues that subjective experience creates a special problem for objective explanation because consciousness has an essentially first-person character that resists capture by third-person scientific description. By **consciousness** we mean the capacity for felt awareness: not merely processing information or behaving intelligently, but having experiences that are present to a subject. Nagel expresses this through the phrase **“what it is like”** to be a creature: if an organism is conscious, then there is something it is like from the inside to be that organism. My thesis is that Nagel is right to identify a genuine explanatory gap between objective accounts and subjective experience, but wrong to suggest that this gap shows an in-principle limit to objective explanation. The problem is real, yet it is better understood as a limitation of our current concepts and methods than as proof that consciousness is forever beyond science.

Nagel’s central claim is that objective explanation works by abstracting away from particular points of view, whereas consciousness is inseparable from a point of view. Science explains phenomena by making them public, measurable, and viewpoint-independent. We explain lightning as electrical discharge, heat as molecular motion, or life in biochemical terms. In each case, the explanation improves by becoming less tied to how things seem to particular observers. But subjective experience moves in the opposite direction. To know that a bat uses echolocation, has certain neural states, and flies at night is not yet to know **what it is like** for the bat to experience the world through sonar. The missing element is not another objective fact of the same kind; it is the bat’s perspective itself. Nagel’s point is therefore not mystical. It is methodological: a complete physical description may leave out the felt character of experience because objective concepts are designed to omit precisely what makes experience subjective.

This challenge is powerful because it exposes a distinction between **function** and **phenomenology**. A theory might explain what a mental state does—how it causes behavior, integrates information, or guides action—without explaining how it feels. A robot could, in principle, discriminate colors, avoid pain stimuli, and report internal states, yet the further question would remain whether there is anything it is like to be that robot. This is why Nagel’s argument is more than an appeal to ignorance. He is identifying a conceptual mismatch. Descartes, despite his dualism, already saw something similar when he treated thought as immediately known in a way bodies are not. Nagel does not embrace Cartesian substance dualism, but he inherits the thought that first-person awareness has a special epistemic status. By contrast, a purely Aristotelian or functional account of the soul as the set of capacities of a living body risks omitting the inner feel of those capacities. Nagel’s insight is that no amount of external description seems automatically to yield the internal aspect.

I agree with Nagel up to this point. There is a real difference between knowing all the public facts about an experience and having that experience. Consider pain. A neurologist may know the exact C-fiber activity, behavioral tendencies, and evolutionary function involved in pain, yet that knowledge is not the same as feeling a migraine. Or consider color vision. One can master the physics of light and the neurobiology of the retina without thereby grasping what red looks like to a normal perceiver if one has never had visual experience. Here Nagel’s argument resembles Mill’s distinction between knowing a description of pleasure and actually appreciating higher pleasures through acquaintance. Some forms of understanding require lived access, not mere propositional knowledge. This supports Nagel’s claim that consciousness cannot be exhausted by objective description as currently understood.

However, it does not follow that subjective experience is in principle beyond objective explanation. A strong counterargument says that Nagel confuses two claims: first, that a third-person description does not **reproduce** an experience; second, that it cannot **explain** it. But explanation need not duplicate its object. A chemical formula does not taste like sugar, yet it explains sweetness in relevant ways. Likewise, a completed neuroscience of consciousness might explain why certain brain processes constitute particular experiences even if reading the theory does not give one those experiences. On this view, Nagel sets the bar too high: he demands that objective science somehow convert third-person knowledge into first-person acquaintance, when explanation need only identify the underlying conditions, structures, and laws.

This objection is serious, but it does not fully defeat Nagel. The analogy with sugar is weaker than it first appears because sweetness is itself known through experience; the chemistry explains the causal basis of sweetness, but it does not eliminate the need for taste as the phenomenon to be explained. With consciousness, the issue is even sharper: the explanandum is precisely the felt aspect. Unless an objective theory can show why and how physical processes are identical with or generate qualitative experience, it risks merely correlating brain states with reports. Kant is relevant here: he argued that objective knowledge concerns appearances structured for possible experience, while the conditions of subjectivity cannot be treated exactly like ordinary objects within experience. Nagel’s point can be read in a Kantian spirit. Subjectivity is not just another item in the world to be viewed from nowhere; it is part of the condition under which a world appears to a subject at all.

Still, I part company with Nagel if his conclusion is taken too pessimistically. Human knowledge often advances by developing new concepts for phenomena once thought inaccessible. Life once seemed irreducible to mechanism; heat seemed unlike motion; heredity seemed mysterious before genetics. Consciousness may demand conceptual innovation rather than surrender. Perhaps future theories will combine first-person data, neuroscience, and computational models in ways that preserve subjectivity without abandoning objectivity. The fact that I cannot know bat experience as a bat does not show that no adequate theory of bat consciousness is possible. It shows only that theory and lived perspective are different kinds of access.

In conclusion, Nagel is right that consciousness presents a distinctive problem because it involves **what it is like** for a subject, and objective explanation typically excludes any particular point of view. His bat example successfully reveals an explanatory gap between public description and private experience. I agree that this gap is philosophically profound and that reductive overconfidence is misplaced. Yet I do not agree that it establishes a permanent barrier to objective understanding. The best position is a qualified one: subjective experience cannot be replaced by objective explanation, but it may still be illuminated by it. Nagel identifies the problem with great force; the mistake is to assume that because present forms of objectivity are incomplete, future ones must be.

# 1. CORE IDEAS (Compressed Understanding)

## Central Claim (Wittgenstein)

* Ethics concerns **absolute value**
* But:

  * Language can only describe **facts**
    → Therefore:
* Ethics **cannot be meaningfully expressed in language**

---

## Key Concepts

### Fact vs Value Distinction

* **Facts**:

  * Descriptions of the world (e.g., “a murder occurred”)
* **Values**:

  * Judgments of worth (e.g., “murder is wrong”)

→ Facts ≠ values 

---

### Relative vs Absolute Value

* **Relative value**:

  * Context-dependent (e.g., “good knife”)
* **Absolute value**:

  * Ultimate, unconditional (e.g., “good life”)

→ Ethics concerns **absolute value**, which cannot be reduced to facts 

---

### Limits of Language (TLP Insight)

* Language represents **facts about the world**
* Ethics concerns what is **outside the world**
  → Ethical statements are:
* **Not false**
* But **nonsensical (cannot be meaningfully stated)** 

---

### “Running against the boundaries of language”

* Attempts to express ethics:

  * Push language beyond its limits
    → Result:
* Failure (logically)
* But:

  * Reveals something important about human life 

---

### Ethics as Expression, Not Knowledge

* Ethics:

  * Does not produce knowledge
  * Expresses a **human attitude toward life** 

---

## Later Wittgenstein: Language-Games

### Language-Game

* Meaning = **use in context**
* Language embedded in:

  * Social practices
  * “Forms of life” 

---

### Key Implication

* Ethical language:

  * Not meaningless
  * But understood through **practice, not theory**

---

## Technology Application (Coeckelbergh)

* Technology is:

  * Embedded in **forms of life**
  * Shaped by values and practices
* Ethics:

  * Emerges from **use, context, interaction**
    → Cannot be fully abstracted or formalized 

---

# 2. ARGUMENT MAPS

## WITTGENSTEIN: ETHICS AND LANGUAGE

### Thesis

* Ethics cannot be meaningfully expressed in language because it concerns **absolute value beyond facts**

---

### Argument Structure

1. Language describes **facts about the world**
2. Ethical judgments concern:

   * Absolute value
   * Meaning of life
3. These are **not facts**
4. Therefore:

   * Ethical statements cannot be expressed meaningfully
5. Conclusion:

   * Ethics lies **outside language**

---

### Key Illustration

* Description of murder:

  * Only facts are described
  * No ethical content is present 

---

### Assumptions

* Meaning = factual representation
* Language limited to describing the world
* Absolute value exists but is ineffable

---

### Strengths

* Explains why ethics feels:

  * Deep
  * Difficult to articulate
* Avoids reduction of ethics to science
* Highlights limits of language

---

### Weaknesses

* If ethics is ineffable:

  * How can we discuss morality at all?
* Self-undermining:

  * Wittgenstein himself talks about ethics
* Too restrictive view of language

---

### Counterarguments

#### Ordinary Language Philosophy

* Ethical language has meaning in practice

#### Moral Realism

* Ethical statements describe real moral facts

#### Pragmatism

* Ethics is about action and consequences, not ineffability

---

## LATER WITTGENSTEIN: LANGUAGE-GAMES

### Thesis

* Meaning is determined by **use within social practices**, not by reference to facts alone

---

### Argument Structure

1. Language is used in many ways (not just describing facts)
2. Meaning depends on:

   * Context
   * Practice
3. Ethical language:

   * Functions within human life
4. Therefore:

   * Ethics is meaningful in practice, even if not scientifically expressible

---

### Strengths

* Explains real-world ethical discourse
* Avoids strict ineffability problem
* Flexible and realistic

---

### Weaknesses

* Risks relativism
* Lacks clear criteria for truth

---

## COECKELBERGH: TECHNOLOGY & LANGUAGE-GAMES

### Thesis

* Ethics of technology must consider **how technologies are embedded in forms of life**

---

### Argument Structure

1. Technology is used within:

   * Social practices
   * Cultural contexts
2. Values are embedded in design and use
3. Therefore:

   * Ethical analysis must focus on **use and context**
4. Conclusion:

   * Ethics is not abstract, but **practical and situated**

---

# 3. ESSAY-READY FRAMES

## Q1. Can we meaningfully talk about ethics?

### Thesis

* Strictly speaking, ethical statements cannot express factual truths, but they remain meaningful within human practices.

### Outline

* Wittgenstein (early): ineffability
* Argument: fact vs value
* Problem: self-undermining
* Later Wittgenstein: language-games
* Conclusion: qualified yes

### Mistakes

* Saying ethics is “meaningless” (too strong)
* Ignoring shift from early to later Wittgenstein

---

## Q2. Does Wittgenstein show that ethics is beyond language?

### Thesis

* Wittgenstein reveals limits of factual language but does not eliminate meaningful ethical discourse.

---

## Q3. What is the role of language-games in ethics?

### Thesis

* Language-games show that ethical meaning arises from social practices rather than abstract truths.

---

## Q4. Is Wittgenstein’s view of ethics self-defeating?

### Thesis

* Yes, because he attempts to express what he claims cannot be expressed, but this paradox reveals the limits of language rather than refuting his view.

---

## Q5. How should we evaluate ethics in technology?

### Thesis

* Ethical evaluation must consider how technologies shape and are shaped by human practices, not just abstract principles.

---

### Later Wittgenstein = “Use Model”

* Meaning = how language is used

---

  * Facts ≠ Values
  * Values = beyond language

* **USE**

  * Understanding
  * Social context
  * Engagement

---

  * Nonsense = cannot be logically expressed
  * Not trivial or useless

* **Early vs Later Wittgenstein**

  * Early: limits
  * Later: use

---

* Ethics still “important” despite being ineffable

# 6. CONNECTIONS & INSIGHT

## Wittgenstein vs Nagel

* Nagel: limits of objective knowledge
* Wittgenstein: limits of language
  → Both:
* Show **boundaries of understanding**

---

## Wittgenstein vs Kant

* Kant: ethics grounded in reason
* Wittgenstein: ethics beyond language/reason

---

## Wittgenstein vs Transhumanism

* Transhumanism:

  * Seeks to engineer ethics
* Wittgenstein:

  * Ethics cannot be reduced to technical description

→ Tension:

* Can we “design” something that cannot be said?

---

## Big Insight

Ethics is not:

* A set of facts
* A scientific theory

It is:

* A **limit case of meaning**
* A reflection of **how we live**
